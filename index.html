Mobile App · UX/UI · 2024

MarketMate — How do you make grocery shopping less painful?

A full end-to-end mobile app design for a multi-supermarket grocery platform, built to help UK shoppers compare prices, scan products, and checkout — all in one place.

01 — The Problem

Shoppers were wasting time jumping between apps

UK grocery shoppers frequently use multiple supermarkets — Tesco, Asda, Morrisons, Lidl — to find the best prices. But there was no single app that let them compare, browse, and buy across all stores. People were toggling between 3–4 apps just to do a weekly shop.

💡
Design Challenge: How might we create a unified grocery experience that feels as simple as shopping at one store, while giving users the power of shopping across many?
02 — Research

Understanding real shopping behaviour

Before opening Figma, I mapped out how people actually shop. I looked at existing apps (Trolley, Google Shopping, individual supermarket apps) to understand what was missing. Key findings shaped the design direction:

1
Users want to see prices side-by-side, not navigate between apps to compare
2
Sustainability is increasingly important — eco-friendly labelling influences purchasing decisions
3
In-store barcode scanning is a feature users wish existed across all supermarket apps
4
Onboarding friction is a drop-off point — users abandon apps that don't personalise quickly
03 — Design Decisions

Every screen had a reason

I structured the app around five core moments in a shopper's journey:

🏠 Home — Personalised feed
Rather than showing everything at once, the home screen surfaces deals and products from stores the user has shopped at before. The Summer Sale banner drives urgency without feeling spammy.
🛒 Personalise — Store selection
Users pick their preferred supermarkets during onboarding. This small step dramatically reduces cognitive load — they only see what's relevant to them.
📋 The Market — Unified product list
Products from all selected stores appear in one scrollable feed, each tagged with the store name and a sustainability badge. Users can add to cart directly without switching contexts.
📷 Scan — In-store product lookup
Pointing the camera at any barcode instantly surfaces product info, price, eco-rating, and a checkout option. This bridges the online/offline gap.
💳 Payment — Clean checkout
Multiple payment methods, saved card info, and a clear receipt screen. Designed to feel as trustworthy as a bank app — no clutter, no confusion.
04 — Outcome

A complete, shippable product

MarketMate went from concept to a fully designed, interactive prototype covering 8+ screens across the complete user journey — onboarding, browsing, scanning, and checkout. The design system used a warm orange brand identity to feel energetic but approachable, distinct from the cold blue/white aesthetics common in grocery apps.

What I learned

Designing for multiple data sources (5 different supermarkets) taught me how important consistent information architecture is. When content comes from many places, the UI has to work extra hard to feel unified. I also learned that sustainability features aren't just nice-to-have — they're a differentiator that makes users feel good about their choices.

Web Design · Redesign · Real Estate · 2024

Meqasa — Redesigning Ghana's #1 property platform

A redesign of Meqasa.com, Ghana's leading real estate marketplace, focused on improving visual hierarchy, search experience, and trust signals to make finding a home feel simple.

01 — The Problem

A powerful platform held back by a dated interface

Meqasa is an established brand with over 40,000 property listings and 2,500+ agents. But the original site had a dense, cluttered layout that made the search experience feel overwhelming — especially for first-time users who are already anxious about finding a home.

💡
Design Challenge: How might we modernise Meqasa's web experience so that finding a property feels calm, trustworthy, and efficient — without losing the depth of listings users rely on?
02 — Audit & Research

What the original site was doing wrong

I conducted a design audit of the live Meqasa site before starting. These were the core issues I identified:

1
Visual hierarchy was flat — everything competed for attention, making it hard to know where to look first
2
The hero search was undersized — the most important action on the page wasn't prominent enough
3
Property cards lacked breathing room — listings felt cramped, making it hard to compare options quickly
4
Trust signals were buried — agent counts and listing numbers that build credibility were hidden in footers
03 — Design Decisions

Clarity over complexity

The redesign focused on three principles: clarity (make the path to finding a home obvious), trust (surface credibility early), and calm (use space and imagery to reduce anxiety around a big life decision).

🏔 Hero — Full-bleed imagery with dominant search
Replaced the original busy header with a full-width property photograph and a large, prominent search bar. The emotional impact of seeing a beautiful home immediately sets the right tone.
🔍 Search filters — Tabbed and scannable
For Rent / For Sale / New Units tabs sit below the hero, keeping the primary navigation visible without cluttering the hero. Filter options (Type, Location, Price, Beds) are clearly spaced and labelled.
🏠 Property cards — More space, better images
Cards were enlarged with bigger photography, cleaner typography, and consistent badge placement (For Sale / For Rent). The result feels more like a design magazine than a classified ads page.
🤝 Trust section — "Work in 3 Easy Steps"
A dedicated section explaining the process (Research → Close the Deal → Key Delivery) addresses user anxiety about the complexity of property transactions in Ghana.
04 — Outcome

Same content, dramatically clearer experience

The redesign preserved everything Meqasa users rely on — the depth of listings, agent network, and category browsing — while making the entire experience feel more premium and less overwhelming. The dark navy and white palette communicates professionalism and trust, appropriate for a platform where people make major financial decisions.

What I learned

Redesigning a live product used by real people is a different challenge from designing from scratch. You have to respect what works and resist the urge to change everything. The biggest lesson was that in high-stakes decisions like buying or renting property, calm and clarity are features. Reducing visual noise is as important as adding new functionality.
